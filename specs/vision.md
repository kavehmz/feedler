# Feedler — Vision

**What this document is:** the WHY. Every product judgment call traces back here. If a question
is "what should this feel like / who is it for / is this worth building", this document answers;
if it is "how is the system shaped / what is the wire contract", `architecture.md` answers.

---

## 1. The problem

Feed readers have not changed in fifteen years, and the recent ones make the wrong bet. A reader
either (a) stays a pure reader — a list, a reading pane, read/unread — and leaves you to do all
the thinking, or (b) bolts a chatbot into the corner: a single hard-wired model, a fixed prompt,
a summarize button you didn't ask for, and no way to use the AI *you* actually trust.

Meanwhile the genuinely useful thing an AI can do with a feed reader is not "chat in a sidebar."
It is: take everything I marked or read today, cluster it, tell me the five things worth my
attention, and link me back to the sources. That is a *batch* job over *my triage*, and it is
best done by whatever frontier model I happen to be using this month — not by a model frozen into
the reader at build time.

## 2. The opportunity

One technical person, their own feeds, their own machine, their own choice of AI. The reader's job
is to make triage fast and to get out of the way; the AI's job is to process the result. The seam
between them should be **plain text you can paste anywhere** — not an API integration, not an
account, not a vendor.

That product is small enough for one person to own completely and valuable enough to use every
day. It does not exist as an off-the-shelf tool, and it is cheap to build for exactly one user.

## 3. Vision

> **Feedler is a fast, keyboard-driven feed reader whose output is a clean Markdown digest of
> your reads — built to hand to any AI, with a link back to both the original article and the
> item in your reader.**

The reader is deliberately conventional and good at the boring parts: import your feeds, fetch
them politely, show you what's unread, let you triage at speed, remember what you've read. The one
opinionated idea is the **export**: instead of embedding an AI, Feedler produces the *input* an AI
needs and lets you choose the AI.

## 4. Value proposition

1. **Triage fast, locally.** Three-pane Reeder-style reader, full keyboard control, read-on-scroll,
   stars, folders. No tutorial; it works the way a feed reader has always worked, only quick.
2. **Own your data.** One SQLite file on your disk. No account, no auth, no cloud, no telemetry.
   Bring it up with one command; delete it with one command.
3. **Export to the AI you choose.** "Today", "This week", a folder, a feed — exported as Markdown
   where **every item carries two links: the original article and a back-link into Feedler** — so
   the AI can cite either, and you can jump from its summary straight back to triage. The AI is
   never inside Feedler; Feedler produces what the AI eats.

## 5. Product pillars

### 5.1 Local-first and single-user

Feedler runs as one process on one machine for one person. There is **no authentication and no
multi-user concept** — not "auth is disabled", but *there is no user model at all*. Every article,
star, and read-mark belongs to the single operator. This is a deliberate scope choice that keeps
the whole system small; adding accounts would change what Feedler *is* (a non-goal, §9).

### 5.2 The export is the AI seam

Feedler holds no API keys, makes no LLM calls, and ships no model. Its contribution to an
AI workflow is a **well-formed Markdown document**: scoped to what you select, bounded by a date
range in *your* timezone, carrying dual links per item. The quality of that document — its scoping,
its links, its grouping, its honesty about what it contains — is the product's signature feature
and is specified with the most care (`components/export_spec.md`).

### 5.3 Polite by default

Feedler fetches other people's servers. It uses conditional requests (ETag / Last-Modified) so it
re-downloads only what changed, caps response sizes, identifies itself with a User-Agent, and
refreshes on a sane interval. A reader that hammers origins is a broken reader.

### 5.4 Trustworthy "already read"

The single most important piece of reader state is *what you have already seen*. Read/unread must
be correct, must survive restarts, and must update the moment you act — on open, on scroll-past, on
"mark all read". If the unread counts lie, the reader is useless. Read-on-scroll is powerful and
divisive, so it is **optional and tunable**, never forced.

### 5.5 Disposable code, durable specs

The reader is simple on purpose, and its code is meant to be thrown away and regenerated by a better
AI from these specs. What is maintained with care is the specification: the data model, the wire
contract, the export format, and the behaviors a user relies on. (`start.md` carries this doctrine.)

## 6. Experience philosophy

- **Conventional where it should be.** A feed reader is a solved interaction; do not reinvent the
  three-pane layout, `j`/`k` navigation, or the unread dot. Familiarity *is* the feature.
- **Fast over featureful.** Every interaction is immediate; the keyboard can drive everything the
  mouse can. A second model in the corner is not a feature — speed and a clean export are.
- **Honest about state.** Unread counts, refresh status, feed errors, and export contents are shown
  truthfully. A feed that 404s says so, and you can retry just that feed. An export tells you how
  many articles it contains and in what timezone the day boundaries were drawn.
- **Two themes, system-aware.** Light and dark are both first-class; the default follows the OS and
  the choice is remembered.

## 7. Audience

- **Primary — the operator, who is also the only user.** One technical person who reads many feeds,
  triages daily, and pipes their reads into an LLM. They run Docker, edit a compose file, and are
  comfortable self-hosting. They set everything; there is no other persona.
- **Anti-persona — the team / the multi-tenant deployment.** Feedler is not a shared service. If the
  question is "how do my colleagues log in", Feedler is the wrong tool by design.

## 8. Success criteria

- **Time to triage:** unread → reviewed in as few keystrokes as possible; the reader never stalls.
- **Export friction ≈ zero:** from "I want today's reads in my LLM" to pasted Markdown in two
  actions (open export, copy).
- **"Already read" is trusted:** the operator never re-reads something the reader claimed was read,
  and counts are never stale after an action.
- **One command up, one command away:** `docker compose up --build` runs the whole thing; the data
  is one file; nothing else is installed on the host.

## 9. Non-goals

1. **Embedded AI.** No built-in chatbot, no server-side LLM calls, no API keys in Feedler. The
   export is the seam; the AI lives outside.
2. **Accounts / auth / multi-user.** No login, no roles, no sharing. Single operator, by design.
3. **A cloud sync service.** Feedler is self-hosted and local; there is no hosted Feedler, no
   account server, no cross-device sync layer.
4. **Native mobile apps.** Web only. (It should be usable in a mobile browser, but no app.)
5. **Analytics / recommendations / a social layer.** No "feeds you might like", no tracking, no
   popularity ranking. The operator curates their own feeds.
6. **A general writing/annotation tool.** Feedler reads and triages and exports; it is not a
   note-taking app or a read-later highlighter.
